Preceptor skills

Reading your student & delivering feedback

Recognize cognitive load, understand how learners receive feedback, and deliver it so it actually sticks

Cognitive load is the bottleneck

Your student's brain has a fixed amount of working memory. When it's full, nothing else gets in — not your teaching, not your feedback, not the thing they already know but can't access right now.

Three types of cognitive load

Understanding which type is consuming your student's bandwidth tells you what to do about it.

Intrinsic load — The inherent difficulty of the task itself. A pediatric respiratory arrest has higher intrinsic load than a lift assist. You can't reduce this, but you can scaffold it.

Extraneous load — Unnecessary cognitive noise that doesn't serve learning. Unclear instructions, distracting environment, equipment they don't know, navigating an unfamiliar crew dynamic. This is the load you can and should eliminate.

Germane load — The productive cognitive work of actually learning. Building mental models, connecting new information to old, making sense of patterns. This is the load you want. Your job is to maximize germane load by reducing extraneous load.

The bandwidth meter

Your student operates at one of three levels at any given moment. Learn to read which one they're in, because it determines what you can ask of them.

Green
Learning zone. They have bandwidth to spare. They can process new information, reflect on what they're doing, and respond to questions. This is where teaching and feedback are most effective. They're calm, methodical, and asking questions themselves.
Amber
Performance zone. They're working at capacity. They can execute what they already know, but there's no room for new concepts. Feedback in this zone gets heard but not processed. Teaching gets lost. They're focused, maybe slightly tunnel-visioned, doing things in the order they learned them.
Red
Overload zone. Working memory is full. They're freezing, repeating themselves, missing obvious things, or going robotic. No learning is happening. Feedback in this zone is literally inaudible — it enters short-term memory and is immediately displaced. They need you to reduce load, not add to it.
⚠
The preceptor trap. Most of us default to teaching when we see a gap. But if your student is in amber or red, teaching adds cognitive load rather than building competence. The most effective thing you can do in those moments is shut up, let them work, and save the teaching for when they have bandwidth to receive it.

What fills the tank

Cognitive load isn't just about call complexity. These factors stack and compound:

Novelty — First time doing a skill, working with a new crew, unfamiliar station or territory.

Emotional intensity — Pediatric patients, death, family distress, conflict on scene.

Decision density — Multiple competing priorities, ambiguous presentations, time pressure.

Social pressure — Being observed, feeling evaluated, performing in front of other crews or hospital staff.

Fatigue — Time of day, sleep quality, shift length. Night four of a block looks different than day one.

Life outside the truck — They may be carrying stress you can't see. A student who was in green zone yesterday might show up in amber today because of something at home.

Reading the signals

Your student won't tell you they're overloaded. They probably don't even know it themselves. You have to learn to see it in their behaviour — and then adjust in real time.

Observable indicators by zone

Green — verbal

Thinking out loud

Narrating their reasoning, asking "what if" questions, connecting current call to previous ones. They're building mental models in real time.

Green — behavioural

Smooth task-switching

Moving between assessment, treatment, and communication without significant pauses. Making eye contact with you. Adapting when something unexpected comes up.

Amber — verbal

Going quiet

Stopped narrating. Answering your questions with short, surface-level responses. Not asking questions themselves. They're conserving bandwidth.

Amber — behavioural

Tunnel vision

Following their mental checklist rigidly. Missing contextual cues. Treating it like a protocol rather than a patient. Slow to respond to changes in the situation.

Red — verbal

Repeating or freezing

Asking the same question twice. Starting a sentence and losing it. Saying "uh..." repeatedly. Or going completely silent for extended periods.

Red — behavioural

Motor breakdown

Fumbling with equipment they normally handle fine. Standing still when they should be acting. Fixating on one task while ignoring higher priorities. Reverting to the first thing they ever learned.

What to do when you read each zone

Green: Teach. Ask questions. Add complexity. "What's the worst thing this could be?" Push them toward the edge of their competence. This is where growth happens.

Amber: Reduce extraneous load. Handle the radio call yourself. Quietly prep equipment so they don't have to multitask. Don't teach — just give brief, clear directives if needed. Save the debrief for later.

Red: Step in. Not as a takeover, but as a load shed. "I've got the monitor, you focus on the airway." Give them one task, make it clear, and reduce their decision space. Debrief much later — they need to decompress first.

ⓘ
Zone transitions are fast. A student can go from green to red in seconds if something unexpected happens — an arrest, an aggressive bystander, equipment failure. Watch for the transition moment, not just the steady state. The shift from narrating to silence is the signal.

Calibrating the challenge

Vygotsky's zone of proximal development is the space between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with support. Your job is to keep them at the edge of that zone — challenged enough to grow, supported enough not to drown.

Most preceptors intervene too early because watching someone struggle is uncomfortable. But productive struggle — the kind that happens in green zone, where they have bandwidth to work through the problem — is exactly where learning lives. Your discomfort isn't a signal to step in. Their zone colour is.

How your student hears feedback

You can deliver perfect feedback and it still won't land if the receiver's brain isn't ready for it. Understanding what happens on their end changes how and when you deliver on yours.

The threat response is the gatekeeper

The moment someone perceives evaluative information, the amygdala fires a threat-or-safety assessment before the prefrontal cortex (the part that actually learns) even gets involved. If the brain reads "threat," it routes to defend, deflect, freeze, or perform agreement. If it reads "safe," the information reaches the learning centres. Your delivery determines which route it takes.

What triggers threat vs. safety

Factor Triggers threat Triggers safety
Timing Feedback in front of patients, crews, or hospital staff Private, after the call, when they've had a minute to breathe
Identity "You're not a strong assessor" (identity-level) "That assessment had gaps" (behaviour-level)
Direction You talk, they listen (lecture mode) You observe, then ask a question (dialogue mode)
Predictability Feedback comes randomly, inconsistently, only when things go wrong Structured debriefs are routine — they expect it every call
Power "I need to talk to you about something" (summons energy) "Let's debrief that one" (normal, expected, low-stakes language)
Cognitive state They're still in amber/red from the call They've returned to green — decompressed, hydrated, sitting down
⚠
The cognitive load connection. If your student is still in amber or red when you start debriefing, the feedback itself becomes additional cognitive load that pushes them further into overload. They'll nod along, say "yeah, okay," and retain nothing. Wait until they're back in green. That might mean waiting until the next posted period, or the drive back to base.

The five SCARF domains

David Rock's model maps five social triggers that the brain processes using the same circuitry as physical threats. Knowing which domain you're activating helps you reframe your delivery.

Status — "Am I being seen as competent?" Threatened by: feedback that implies incompetence, comparisons to other students.

Certainty — "Do I know what's expected?" Threatened by: vague feedback, unclear standards, shifting goalposts.

Autonomy — "Do I have agency?" Threatened by: being told exactly what to do, micromanagement, no choices.

Relatedness — "Am I part of the team?" Threatened by: feeling singled out, excluded, or judged as an outsider.

Fairness — "Is this fair?" Threatened by: inconsistent standards, being called out for something others get away with.

Reading their receiving style

Pay attention in the first few shifts to how your student responds to feedback. People have default patterns:

The deflector: "Yeah but..." followed by an explanation. They're protecting status. Frame feedback as building on what's already working.

The absorber: Goes quiet, nods, says nothing. They might be processing deeply, or they might be shutting down. Follow up later: "I shared some observations earlier — what landed and what didn't?"

The performer: "Absolutely, totally, I'll fix that right away." Quick agreement without depth. They want to please. Slow them down: "Before you jump to fixing it, tell me what you think caused it."

The questioner: Asks clarifying questions, wants specifics. They're genuinely processing. Give them the detail they need — they'll do the work.

Delivering feedback that sticks

One message, anchored to something specific, connected to an outcome. Positive or corrective — never both at the same time.

Why the feedback sandwich fails

Positive-negative-positive teaches your student to brace for the "but" every time you say something good. It dilutes both messages, erodes trust in your praise, and trains them to filter for the criticism in the middle. You end up delivering three messages and landing zero.

The rule: one type per conversation

If you have corrective feedback, give it cleanly. If you have positive feedback, let it stand on its own so it registers as genuine. Separate them in time — even a few calls apart is enough. Your student needs to know that when you say "that was good," there's no "but" coming.

The OIQ model

Three components. Works for both positive and corrective feedback. The anchor is the observation — the more specific it is, the more useful everything after it becomes.

O

Observation

What you specifically saw or heard. Behavioural, concrete, verifiable. No interpretation, no judgment. This is your anchor — and the part that's hardest when your memory doesn't cooperate. Jot one line on your phone right after the call: 10 words, the specific thing. That note is your debrief ammunition later.

I

Impact

Connect the behaviour to an outcome — patient outcome, clinical standard, team function, or learning goal. "Here's why that mattered." This is where the feedback becomes meaningful rather than arbitrary. A behaviour without a connected outcome feels like a preference, not a principle.

Q

Question

Open the door to their perspective. This is the step most people skip, and it's the most important one. "What was going on for you in that moment?" Without it, feedback is a lecture. With it, you discover the root cause — and each root cause requires a different intervention.

✓
The phone note hack. Right after the call, while the student is doing paperwork, type one line into your Notes app: the specific behaviour and whether it was good or a gap. "Didn't reassess after ventolin" or "Directed fire without hesitation." That's your observation, pre-captured. The impact and question you can construct later because those come from your clinical knowledge, not your memory of the event.

Positive vs. corrective: what changes

Element Positive example Corrective example
Observation "You repositioned the patient before reassessing vitals, without being prompted" "You didn't auscultate lung sounds before or after the ventolin"
Impact "That tells me you're thinking about interventions as a cycle, not a checklist — that's the clinical thinking we want" "Without a baseline, we can't determine if the intervention worked — and it's a BLS PCS standard"
Question "What tipped you off that repositioning might help?" "Help me understand — is it a prioritization thing or is it not on your radar for these patients?"

Timing: hot vs. cold

Hot debrief (right after the call): Best for immediate factual correction and emotional processing. Keep it under 2 minutes. One point only. Only do this if they're back in green zone — not while they're still completing the ACR or decompressing.

Cold debrief (end of shift, posted period, next day): Best for pattern recognition and deeper reflection. The learner has distance, you've had time to choose your words, and you can connect multiple experiences. "Over the last three calls, I've noticed..."

Rule of thumb: Single-call corrective feedback? Hot, if they have bandwidth. Pattern feedback? Always cold. Positive feedback? Either, but immediate reinforcement has the strongest effect.

ⓘ
Permission-based feedback. For non-urgent corrective feedback, try: "Can I share an observation from that call?" This small act of consent shifts the dynamic from evaluation to collaboration. It doesn't work for patient safety issues — those get addressed immediately — but for developmental feedback it puts the student in a receptive state rather than a defensive one.

Scenario practice

Write your response before revealing the example. Generating your own answer — even a rough one — is retrieval practice, and it's where the learning actually cements.

Scenario 1 — Reading cognitive load

You're on a call with a 78-year-old in respiratory distress. Your student (week 4) started strong — good primary, appropriate vitals. Then the family starts shouting questions, the patient's SpO2 drops to 84%, and you notice your student has stopped talking, is staring at the monitor, and hasn't reached for the BVM. What's happening and what do you do?

Zone shift: Green to red. The compounding factors — desaturating patient, family distress, and decision pressure — overwhelmed working memory simultaneously. The silence and fixation on the monitor are classic red-zone indicators.

Immediate action: Shed load. "I'm going to talk to the family — you grab the BVM and get that sat up." One task, one clear directive, removal of the social stressor. You're not taking over the call; you're reducing their decision space to one actionable item.

After the call: Don't debrief immediately — they need to decompress. Later, when they're back in green: "I noticed you went quiet when the sat dropped and the family got loud. That's a normal overload response. Let's talk about what you can do when you feel that happening."

Key insight: The debrief is about building their self-awareness of their own overload signals, not about the BVM delay. If they learn to recognize their own red zone, they can self-manage next time.
Scenario 2 — Corrective feedback with OIQ

Your student didn't check a blood glucose on an altered LOC patient. This is the second time this has happened. They're generally progressing well and you have a good relationship. Build your feedback using OIQ.

O: "On both the call this morning and the one Tuesday, the BGL wasn't part of your workup on an altered patient."

I: "Hypoglycemia is the most reversible cause of altered LOC, and missing it delays a treatment that takes seconds. It's also a BLS PCS standard — it needs to be reflexive, not something you have to think about."

Q: "Help me understand what's happening — is it getting bumped by other priorities, or is it not on your radar for altered presentations?"

The question matters because the root cause changes the intervention. If it's a prioritization problem, you work on their assessment sequence. If it's a knowledge gap (they don't associate glucose with altered LOC), you teach the pathophysiology. You can't coach what you don't understand.
Scenario 3 — Positive feedback with OIQ

Your student just ran a pediatric call. Without prompting, they got down to the child's level, used age-appropriate language, and kept the parent in the loop. The child stayed calm through the entire assessment.

O: "You got down to her level, used words like 'listening to your chest' instead of medical terminology, and kept mom updated at every step."

I: "That kid stayed calm the entire time. When a pediatric patient is calm, your assessment quality goes way up — you got better lung sounds, a more accurate respiratory rate, and a more useful clinical picture than most crews would have."

Q: "Was that deliberate or did it just happen naturally? I want to understand what was going through your head."

Delivered on its own — no "but" coming. The specificity tells them exactly what to repeat. The question helps them become conscious of an unconscious competence so they can replicate it deliberately. And no, this is not the moment to also mention that their radio report was weak. That's a separate conversation.
Scenario 4 — Timing and receiving style

Your student is a "performer" — they always respond to feedback with quick agreement ("Totally, yep, I'll fix that"). You've noticed the same medication administration error three times. You've given feedback twice and gotten enthusiastic agreement both times, but nothing changed. What's your approach this time?

Name the pattern: "I've brought this up twice before and you agreed to work on it both times. It's still happening. I don't think the issue is willingness — I think something else is going on that we haven't identified yet."

Slow them down: "Before you tell me you'll fix it, I want you to walk me through exactly what happened in your head during the med admin. Step by step, starting from when you pulled the drug."

Why this works: You're bypassing the performance response by explicitly naming it and redirecting to a process-level conversation. The step-by-step narration forces them to actually reconstruct the event instead of jumping to the resolution. You'll likely discover the root cause in the narration — a knowledge gap, a sequencing error, or an environmental factor they haven't articulated.

The meta-message: You're also teaching them that quick agreement without understanding isn't what you're looking for. You want genuine engagement with the problem.

On shift

The application layer. A shift-by-shift checklist and quick-reference models you can pull up on the truck.

Your shift checklist

Pick one or two per shift. After you've cycled through them all, they become automatic. Click to check off.

  • Read your student's zone at the start of shift — where are they today?
  • During a call, consciously identify their cognitive load zone at least once
  • After a call, jot a 10-word note on your phone before doing anything else
  • Give one piece of positive feedback using OIQ — on its own, no "but"
  • Give one piece of corrective feedback using OIQ — separate from any positive
  • On a green-zone call, ask one Socratic question instead of giving an answer
  • On an amber/red call, resist the urge to teach in the moment — save it for later
  • Let your student struggle 10 extra seconds before stepping in
  • Notice your student's receiving pattern (deflector, absorber, performer, questioner)
  • Ask for permission before non-urgent corrective feedback: "Can I share an observation?"
