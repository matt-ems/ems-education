Burns Emergency

Thermal, Chemical & Electrical Burn Management for Ontario PCPs. The burns you can see are often not the burns that kill.

📟 Dispatch — 0247h

"Respond to a residential structure fire — one patient self-extricated, conscious and breathing, minor burns."

You arrive to find fire crews working a kitchen fire. Your patient is a 52-year-old male sitting on the porch. He tells you the grease fire on the stove flared up and he tried to smother it with a towel. The kitchen filled with thick black smoke. He was in the room for "maybe five minutes" before getting out.

His forearms have red, blistering skin where the grease splashed. He's coughing occasionally. His voice sounds a little raspy, but he's talking in full sentences.

Vitals: HR 92 · RR 20 · SpO₂ 98% · BP 134/82

His neighbour says: "He seems fine — he walked out on his own."

SpO₂ is 98%. Vitals are normal. He's talking to you.
So why should this call scare you more than the burns on his arms?

This module will teach you why the burns you can see are often not the burns that kill — and how to recognize, assess, and manage the full spectrum of burn injuries using the Ontario BLS Patient Care Standards.

The Why — Burn Pathophysiology That Matters on the Truck

You don't need a physiology textbook to manage burns. You need to understand three things: how skin protects you, what happens when it fails, and why that makes your patient sick in ways you can't always see.

What Skin Actually Does for You›

Barrier: Keeps bacteria out and fluids in. When skin is destroyed, plasma leaks out and pathogens walk in. This is why large burns cause hypovolaemia and infection — the barrier is gone.

Thermoregulation: Skin controls body temperature through vasodilation, vasoconstriction, and sweating. Burned skin can't do any of this. That's why your burn patient gets hypothermic — and why hypothermia in burn patients is a clinical emergency, not just discomfort.

Sensation: Pain receptors in the dermis tell the patient (and you) something is wrong. Full-thickness burns destroy these receptors. A painless burn is a deep burn — that's information, not reassurance.

Fluid regulation: Intact capillaries keep plasma in the vessels. Burn injury causes massive capillary permeability — fluid pours into the interstitial space (oedema). This happens both locally and systemically in large burns.

Burn Depth — What It Looks Like and What It Means›
DepthWhat You SeeWhat It Means
Superficial (1st)Red, dry, painful. No blisters. Think sunburn.Only epidermis damaged. Barrier intact. Heals on its own. NOT counted in TBSA estimates.
Partial-thickness (2nd)Red, wet, blistered, very painful. Blanches with pressure.Epidermis destroyed, dermis partially damaged. Pain receptors intact (that's why it hurts). Barrier compromised — fluid loss and infection risk are real.

The Airway Problem — Why Smoke Kills Before Burns Do›

The upper airway (above the vocal cords) is incredibly efficient at absorbing heat. Hot gases from a fire cause thermal injury to the supraglottic structures — the epiglottis, aryepiglottic folds, and false vocal cords. These structures swell. Fast.

Why this matters on the truck: Upper airway oedema can progress from "hoarse voice" to "complete obstruction" in 30–60 minutes. By the time you see stridor, you're behind.

Below the cords: The lower airway is usually protected from direct thermal injury. However, inhaled toxic products of combustion (carbon monoxide, hydrogen cyanide, particulate matter) cause chemical injury to the lower airways and alveoli, leading to bronchospasm, pulmonary oedema, and impaired gas exchange.

Carbon monoxide (CO): CO binds to haemoglobin with 200× the affinity of oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb). The pulse oximeter reads COHb as oxyhaemoglobin. A SpO₂ of 98% can be a lie. High-concentration oxygen is the treatment because it displaces CO from haemoglobin — the half-life of COHb drops from ~4-6 hours on room air to ~60-90 minutes on 100% O₂.

Key Clinical Takeaway
A normal SpO₂ does NOT rule out carbon monoxide poisoning. Standard pulse oximetry cannot distinguish carboxyhaemoglobin from oxyhaemoglobin. After any enclosed-space fire, treat the airway and the blood — not the number on the monitor.

High Risk — Who Gets Burned and Why It Matters

Knowing who is at higher risk for burn injury — and why — changes your history-taking from a checklist into a clinical tool.

Paediatric Patients›

Why they're at risk: Thinner skin means the same temperature causes deeper burns faster. Higher body surface area-to-mass ratio means they lose heat and fluid proportionally faster. Scalds (hot liquid spills) are the most common mechanism in children under 5.

What to ask: Mechanism and timing. Was the child unattended? Does the burn pattern match the story? Immersion burns with clear lines of demarcation (glove/stocking pattern) or burns to buttocks/perineum should raise concern for non-accidental injury.

What to watch for: Hypothermia develops faster. Use the modified Rule of Nines — the child's head is 18% total body surface area (TBSA) vs 9% in adults, each lower extremity is 14% vs 18%.

Geriatric Patients›

Why they're at risk: Thinner skin (dermal atrophy with aging) means minor mechanisms cause significant burns. Decreased mobility means longer exposure time. Pre-existing conditions (diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, heart failure) impair healing and reduce physiological reserve.

What to ask: Medications — anticoagulants complicate wound care. Baseline mobility and cognitive status. Was there a fall or syncopal event that preceded the burn?

What to watch for: Hypotension may appear earlier due to reduced cardiac reserve.

Industrial / Occupational Exposures›

Why they're at risk: High-energy mechanisms (electrical, chemical, flash burns) cause injuries disproportionate to surface appearance. Chemical burns may involve ongoing tissue destruction.

What to ask: What substance? What voltage/current type? Duration of contact? Loss of consciousness? Is a Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) available?

What to watch for: Electrical injuries — entry/exit wounds may look small but internal damage can be massive. Chemical burns — know your decontamination requirements.

Patients with Pre-existing Conditions›

Diabetes: Peripheral neuropathy means they may not feel a burn developing (contact burns from heating pads, hot water). Microvascular disease impairs perfusion and healing.

Peripheral vascular disease: Already compromised distal circulation makes compartment syndrome more dangerous.

Substance use: Impaired judgment leads to longer exposure. Altered sensation delays recognition. Co-intoxication complicates assessment.

BLS PCS Anchor
The BLS PCS v3.4, Section 3 — Burns (Thermal) Standard requires you to determine the source of burn, whether the fire occurred in an enclosed space, and whether the patient lost consciousness during exposure. These aren't just documentation — they're clinical decision tools that tell you about inhalation injury risk and burn severity.
● Recall
You respond to a kitchen scald injury involving a 68-year-old woman with diabetes and peripheral neuropathy. She placed her hand in hot dishwater and didn't realize it was scalding until she noticed blistering. What is the MOST important clinical implication of her neuropathy for this burn?
Correct. Neuropathy means she didn't feel the pain that would normally cause instant withdrawal. Longer exposure time = deeper burn. A burn that looks partial-thickness may in fact be full-thickness underneath because the exposure was prolonged. This is why "duration of exposure" is one of the history questions that matters most — it directly predicts burn depth.
The key issue is exposure time. Neuropathy eliminated the pain reflex that normally causes instant withdrawal from a hot surface. She stayed in contact with the hot water longer than a patient with intact sensation would have. Longer exposure = deeper burn. This is why the history question "how long was the exposure?" is clinically critical — it predicts burn depth even when the surface appearance is ambiguous.

Burn Severity Assessment (BLS PCS Required)

The BLS PCS requires you to estimate three things for every burn:

1. Area Burned — Location and Pattern›

Location matters. Face, hands, feet, genitalia, and major joints are critical areas regardless of TBSA. Circumferential burns to extremities or the chest are high-risk for compartment syndrome and respiratory compromise.

2. Burn Depth — Superficial, Partial, or Full›

Multiple depths can coexist in the same patient. Remember: painless ≠ minor. A painless burn means full-thickness destruction of nerve endings.

3. TBSA Percentage — The Rule of Nines›

Use the Rule of Nines (adult) or Modified Rule of Nines (paediatric). Only count partial-thickness and full-thickness burns — superficial burns are NOT included.

Body RegionAdultChild
Head & Neck9%18%
Each Upper Extremity9%9%
Anterior Torso18%18%
Posterior Torso18%18%
Each Lower Extremity18%14%
Perineum1%1%

Quick tip: The patient's palm (including fingers) ≈ 1% TBSA. Useful for estimating scattered or irregular burns.

Why TBSA matters for management: The 15% TBSA threshold drives a critical dressing decision in the BLS PCS — partial-thickness burns below 15% get moist sterile dressing, at or above 15% they get dry sterile dressing. Getting this number right changes your treatment.

Inhalation Injury Assessment (BLS PCS Required)

Signs of Smoke Inhalation & Upper Airway Injury›

Airway signs: Burns to lips/mouth, singed nasal hair or eyebrows, facial burns, carbon particles in saliva/sputum, stridor or hoarseness, drooling.

Respiratory signs: Cough, shortness of breath, shallow respirations, decreased air entry, audible wheezes, tachypnoea.

Any ONE of these after fire/smoke exposure = suspected inhalation injury.

Distal Neurovascular Status (BLS PCS Required)

For any burned extremity, assess pulse, sensation, motor function, and capillary refill distal to the burn. Re-check approximately every 10 minutes if compromised.

Key Clinical Takeaway
Eye burns: assess as per the Visual Disturbance Standard, BUT if the eye is swollen shut, leave it shut. Do not force open a swollen burned eyelid.

●● Application
You are assessing a patient with burns from a propane grill explosion. You find circumferential full-thickness burns to the entire right forearm. Distal pulses are present now. What is the MOST important reason to reassess distal neurovascular status frequently?
Correct. Full-thickness burned skin (eschar) is leather-like and cannot expand. As tissue beneath swells from the inflammatory response, pressure builds in a closed compartment — creating a tourniquet effect. This is compartment syndrome. If uncorrected, it causes ischaemia distal to the burn. Pulses present now does not mean pulses present in 20 minutes.

As tissue swells beneath, pressure rises in a closed space, compressing blood vessels — like a tourniquet. This is why the BLS PCS requires assessment of distal neurovascular status in burned extremities. Present pulses now ≠ present pulses later.

The Clock — How Burn Injuries Progress

Burns are dynamic injuries. Your patient's condition at 5 minutes post-burn is not the same as at 30 minutes or 2 hours. Understanding the timeline tells you where your patient is and where they're headed.

Stage 1 — The "Looks Okay" Window (0–30 min)

Patient shows: Pain, anxiety, possibly coughing. May be ambulatory. Talking. Vitals often normal or mildly tachycardic.

What you see: Surface burns are obvious. Airway may sound clear or have mild hoarseness. SpO₂ may read normal.

What it means: This is the deceptive phase. Airway oedema is developing but not yet critical. Fluid shifts are beginning but haven't caused hypotension. CO is bound to haemoglobin but the patient may look pink. This is your window to act — before the patient declares.

⚠ Stage 2 — The Danger Zone (30 min – 2 hours)

Patient shows: Increasing hoarseness, progressive stridor, worsening cough, agitation. Tachycardia increasing. May develop hypotension with large burns.

What you see: Voice changes become obvious. Facial/neck swelling. Wheezing on auscultation. Burns may look "worse" as blisters develop and partial-thickness burns deepen.

What it means: Airway oedema is becoming significant. Fluid is pouring out of the vascular space. This is where "walking and talking" becomes "stridorous and deteriorating."

🚨 Stage 3 — Decompensation (2+ hours)

Patient shows: Severe stridor or loss of voice. Marked hypotension. Altered mental status. Hypothermia developing.

What you see: Critically ill patient. Airway near-complete obstruction. Burns that looked partial-thickness may now appear full-thickness. Mottled skin, delayed cap refill, absent distal pulses.

What it means: Every system is failing. Early aggressive management in Stage 1 is what prevents this stage.

The Trap
Stage 1 looks reassuring. Stage 3 looks catastrophic. The critical skill is recognizing Stage 1 patients headed for Stage 3. Facial burns + enclosed space + any airway sign = treat as if Stage 2 is imminent, even if the patient looks fine right now.

Management — What to Do and Why It Works

Every intervention has a physiological reason. Knowing WHY you're doing something means you can adapt when reality doesn't match the textbook.

Immediate Priorities

Scene Safety & Stop the Burning Process›

Action: If in a smoke/fume-filled environment, request fire personnel. Move patient to fresh air when safe.

Why: Continued exposure = continued injury. Every second in a toxic environment adds to inhalation injury and burn depth.

Action: Stop the burning process. Remove clothing — but cut around clothing adherent to skin.

Why: Adherent clothing is stuck to destroyed tissue. Pulling it off removes viable tissue and worsens the wound.

Airway & Oxygen›

Action: High-concentration oxygen for any suspected CO or cyanide exposure — which means ANY fire involving combustible materials.

Why: 100% O₂ accelerates CO displacement from haemoglobin. COHb half-life drops from ~4-6 hours (room air) to ~60-90 minutes (100% O₂).

Action: For facial burns, place gauze pads under the edges of the oxygen mask.

Why: Mask edges pressing on burned skin causes pain and further tissue damage.

Dressing Selection — The Critical Decision Tree

The BLS PCS has specific dressing rules based on burn depth and TBSA. Getting this wrong leads to either inadequate cooling or hypothermia:

Burn TypeDressingRationale
1st degree (superficial)Moist sterile, then dry sheet/blanketCooling provides pain relief. No significant fluid loss risk.
2nd degree <15% TBSAMoist sterile, then dry sheet/blanketCooling helps. Fluid loss manageable at this TBSA.
2nd degree ≥15% TBSADry sterile dressing or sheetMoist dressings over large areas = hypothermia. Heat loss through damaged skin is already significant at ≥15%.
3rd degree (full-thickness)Dry sterile dressing or sheetFull-thickness burns don't benefit from cooling (nerve endings destroyed). Dry dressing prevents heat loss.
Key Clinical Takeaway

The 15% TBSA line is the critical threshold. Below it: moist is fine. At or above it: dry only. This single decision point prevents hypothermia — a leading killer in burn patients.

Additional PCS rules: Cool burns <15% TBSA only, limit cooling to <30 minutes. If remoistening is needed, remove the dry cover and remoisten the sterile dressing underneath. Dress digits individually. Leave blisters intact.

Critical Safety Rule
If shivering or hypotension develops, discontinue ALL cooling efforts immediately. Switch to dry sterile dressing. Keep the patient warm. Hypothermia impairs coagulation, worsens acidosis, and increases mortality.
Chemical Burns — Decontamination Rules›

The BLS PCS Hazardous Materials Injury Standard has specific irrigation times:

Chemical TypeMin. Irrigation at Scene
Alkali (known or suspected)20 minutes if stable
Acid (known)10 minutes if stable
Unknown chemical20 minutes if stable
Water-reactive chemicalDo NOT irrigate

Powder/solid chemicals: Brush off or manually remove first → confirm not water-reactive → irrigate with cool (not cold) water.

Eye irrigation: Affected side down (one eye) or supine (both eyes). Manually open eyelids if needed. Irrigate away from tear duct(s). Patient removes contact lenses if readily removable.

Electrical Injury — Hidden Damage›

Scene safety first: Never touch a potential energized source or patient still in contact.

Assess for: Entry/exit wounds, dysrhythmias, burns, muscle spasms, cold/mottled/pulseless extremities, neurologic impairment, shallow/irregular respirations.

Why electrical is different: Electricity follows nerves, blood vessels, and muscle. Internal muscle necrosis can be massive while skin burns appear minor. Cardiac dysrhythmias can be immediate or delayed.

Determine: AC vs DC current and voltage. AC is generally more dangerous — tetanic contraction means the patient can't let go.

ACP Scope — Pain Management
ACPs have access to the Analgesia Medical Directive. Options: fentaNYL (IV/IN, up to 75 mcg/dose, max 200 mcg cumulative), morphine (IV/SC, up to 10 mg/dose, max 20 mg cumulative), ketamine (IV/IN). Ketamine is preferred for hypotensive patients — no haemodynamic effects. IV fluid therapy (0.9% NaCl, 20 ml/kg bolus) available for hypotension.

What NOT To Do

Common Mistakes That Cause Harm

Do NOT apply ice or ice water. Ice causes vasoconstriction and accelerates hypothermia.

Do NOT pop blisters. Intact blisters are a sterile biological dressing.

Do NOT apply creams, butter, toothpaste. They trap heat, contaminate wounds, and interfere with hospital assessment.

Do NOT remove clothing stuck to burns. Cut around it.

Do NOT underestimate enclosed-space fires. Inhalation injury is the leading cause of fire-related death.

The Traps — Myths, Mimics & Missed Calls

The deepest learning happens when your assumptions get challenged. Tap each myth to confront what you think you know.

Myth Busters

Myth #1

"SpO₂ is 98% — the patient is oxygenating fine."

Tap to reveal →

Reality: Standard pulse oximetry cannot distinguish oxyhaemoglobin from carboxyhaemoglobin. After CO exposure, SpO₂ of 98% may mean 70% O₂ and 28% CO on haemoglobin. The patient is profoundly hypoxic despite the reassuring number. Treat the mechanism, not the monitor.

Myth #2

"Third-degree burns are the most painful — they're the worst ones."

Tap to reveal →

Reality: Full-thickness (3rd degree) burns are painless — nerve endings are destroyed. The most painful burns are partial-thickness (2nd degree), where nerve endings are exposed but intact. A patient reporting NO pain in a burn area is giving you clinical information: that burn is deeper than it looks.

Myth #3

"Small burns can't cause shock — there isn't enough fluid loss."

Tap to reveal →

Reality: Hypovolaemic shock from fluid loss alone requires larger burns (typically >20% TBSA). But "small" burns CAN cause shock through other mechanisms: electrical injury → cardiac dysrhythmias → cardiogenic shock. Inhalation injury → distributive shock. Pain → neurogenic/vasovagal response. Assess the whole patient, not just the skin.
