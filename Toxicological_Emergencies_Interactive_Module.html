Toxicological Emergencies

You don't need to be a toxicologist. You need to be a systematic assessor who protects the airway, anticipates deterioration, and gathers the intel that saves lives downstream.

📟 Dispatch

0247h — "Unknown medical, 23-year-old male, found unresponsive in apartment."

You walk in. The patient is on the bathroom floor, snoring respirations, GCS 6. His buddy says he was "partying" and went quiet about 30 minutes ago. There's a small baggie of white powder on the counter. No medication bottles visible. SpO₂ reads 91% and falling.

What are you thinking? What do you do first? What are you preparing for?

Keep this scene in the back of your mind as you work through this module. By the end, you'll have a systematic framework for this call — and every tox call after it.

Why This Matters on the Truck

Tox calls are unpredictable. The substance may be unknown, the patient can't tell you what they took, and the clinical picture changes fast. But here's the key insight:

Toxins don't invent new problems — they attack the same body systems you already know how to assess.

Your BLS assessment framework already gives you every tool you need. This module will show you why each step matters and help you connect the dots between the toxin and what's happening to your patient.

🧠 Check Yourself
Back to our dispatch call: the patient is unresponsive (GCS 6) with snoring respirations and SpO₂ of 91%. What is your immediate first priority?
Airway first, always. A GCS of 6 with snoring respirations means this patient has lost protective reflexes. Identifying the substance matters — but a dead patient can't benefit from detective work. Open the airway, suction if needed, and ventilate. BLS PCS: Altered LOC Standard, p. 72
Not quite. With a GCS of 6 and snoring respirations, this patient's airway is at immediate risk. Everything else can wait until the airway is managed. Remember the cascade: CNS depression → lost reflexes → airway compromise → respiratory arrest → cardiac arrest. Interrupt it early.

Step 1: The Detective Work

Before you can predict what's going to go wrong, you need to understand what got in and how it got in.

BLS PCS p. 101, Items 1–2

The BLS Toxicological Emergency Standard opens with intelligence gathering for a reason. You're trying to answer four questions: What agent? How much? When? Which route?

Route of Exposure — Speed Matters

The route tells you how fast the toxin will hit and which systems get targeted first.

RouteSpeedWhy It Matters
Injection / IVSeconds–minutesBypasses all barriers. Hits the bloodstream immediately. Fastest onset, hardest to reverse.
InhalationSeconds–minutesMassive lung surface area → rapid absorption. Think CO, fentanyl vapour, volatile chemicals.
IngestionMinutes–hoursMust pass through the GI tract. Onset depends on stomach contents and formulation. Extended-release is a trap.
AbsorptionMinutes–hoursSlower but insidious. Patient may not realize they've been exposed. Think organophosphates, fentanyl powder, chemical spills.
Clinical Pearl

The prescription bottle check — BLS item 2 (p. 101) asks you to find the prescription date and count what's left. If a patient was prescribed 60 tablets of 1 mg lorazepam two days ago and only 10 remain, you've just estimated the dose without the patient saying a word: 50 tablets × 1 mg = 50 mg ingested. That's what you communicate to the receiving facility. Be the investigator.

⚠️ Common Trap

"The patient says they only took a couple of pills, so it's probably not serious."

Patients under-report, especially intentional overdoses. Always verify with the pill count. And with extended-release formulations (look for XR, ER, or CR on the label), a patient who looks stable now can crash hours later as the drug continues to absorb.

🧠 Check Yourself
You arrive at a call for a 45-year-old female. Her husband says she swallowed "a bunch of pills" about 90 minutes ago. She's currently alert but drowsy. You find a nearly empty bottle of extended-release verapamil (a calcium channel blocker) prescribed 5 days ago, 30 tablets, 4 remaining. The label reads "verapamil ER" — look for XR, ER, or CR on the bottle, which all mean extended-release. Why should you be more concerned, not less, that she's currently stable?
Extended-release formulations are a trap. The drug is designed to release slowly over hours. This patient may have ~26 tablets of verapamil on board and the peak effect hasn't arrived yet. Anticipate cardiovascular collapse — profound bradycardia and hypotension. This is a time-critical transport. Communicate the estimated dose and formulation type to the receiving facility early.
The key issue is the extended-release formulation. These drugs release over hours, meaning the full dose hasn't been absorbed yet. A "stable" patient with an ER overdose can deteriorate rapidly. The estimated 26-tablet ingestion of a calcium channel blocker is a life-threatening dose regardless of current presentation.

Step 2: Body Systems Under Attack

Toxins attack four systems. Know them, and you can anticipate what's coming before it arrives.

BLS PCS p. 101, Item 4

The BLS Tox Standard lists specific problems to prepare for. This isn't a random list — it's organized around the four body systems that toxins most commonly attack. Click each system below to learn the mechanism.

What you'll find: Altered or changing LOC, agitation, confusion, sudden violent behaviour, seizures. GCS may be fluctuating.

Why it happens: The brain is exquisitely sensitive to chemical disruption. Depressants (opioids, benzos, alcohol) enhance inhibitory neurotransmitters like GABA — the CNS slows down through drowsiness, unconsciousness, and eventually respiratory arrest. Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines) flood the brain with catecholamines, causing agitation, psychosis, hyperthermia, and seizures.

The BLS specifically asks you to watch for "sudden violent behaviour" — that's recognizing sympathomimetic toxicity.

Clinical Pearl

A decreasing LOC means the depressant is winning. An escalating agitation means the stimulant is winning. Both paths lead to the same endpoint: airway compromise. Protect the airway early.

What you'll find: Respiratory distress, apnoea, hypoventilation, airway obstruction (loss of gag reflex, emesis, bronchospasm).

Three mechanisms of respiratory failure in poisoning:

1. Central depression — opioids and sedatives suppress the brainstem respiratory centre. The drive to breathe simply shuts off.

2. Airway obstruction — the patient loses gag reflex, vomits, and aspirates. Or the tongue falls back and blocks the airway.

3. Bronchospasm / chemical injury — inhaled toxins cause direct irritation, swelling, or spasm of the airways.

The BLS listing three separate respiratory problems (obstruction, arrest, distress) reflects these three distinct mechanisms.

Clinical Pearl

Opioid overdose is the classic respiratory killer. The patient doesn't stop breathing suddenly — they breathe slower and shallower over time. Count the rate. If it's dropping, the clock is ticking.

What you'll find: Dysrhythmias, tachycardia, bradycardia, hypotension, hypertension, cardiac arrest.

Why it happens: Stimulants cause catecholamine surges → dangerously high HR and BP → dysrhythmias and stroke. Depressants and cardiac toxins (beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, TCAs) slow conduction, widen the QRS, and cause fatal dysrhythmias or profound hypotension. Some toxins cause vasodilation → distributive shock.

Clinical Pearl

Cardiac arrest in a young, previously healthy patient with no trauma? Think toxicological cause. These arrests are potentially reversible if you identify the agent and communicate it early. The Cardiac Arrest Standard (p. 74) directs you to "consider reversible causes" — poisoning is a key one.

What you'll find: Emesis (major airway threat), hyperthermia, hypothermia, abdominal distension or tenderness.

Emesis is the body trying to eject an ingested toxin — protective, but extremely dangerous in a patient with decreased LOC. Emesis + no gag reflex = aspiration. The BLS listing emesis as a potential problem is really saying: this is an airway threat in disguise.

Hyperthermia from sympathomimetics (cocaine, MDMA, amphetamines) isn't a fever — it's a metabolic fire. Excessive catecholamines drive muscle activity and metabolic rate beyond the body's ability to cool itself. Serotonin syndrome and anticholinergic toxicity also disrupt thermoregulation.

Clinical Pearl

Have suction ready before the patient vomits, not after. In a decreased-LOC tox patient, position and prepare. Emesis is when, not if.

🧠 Check Yourself
Your patient, a 30-year-old male, is increasingly agitated after using an unknown stimulant. His HR is 142, BP 188/110, pupils are dilated, skin is hot and diaphoretic, and temperature is 39.8°C. Which body system failure should you be most anticipating next?
This is a classic sympathomimetic toxidrome. The catecholamine surge is driving HR, BP, and temperature dangerously high. Seizures are the next domino — stimulants lower the seizure threshold, and hyperthermia accelerates the process. After seizures: cardiac dysrhythmias, stroke, and cardiac arrest. Prepare for seizures and prioritize rapid transport. BLS PCS: Seizure Standard, p. 96
Look at the whole picture: agitation, tachycardia, hypertension, dilated pupils, hyperthermia, diaphoresis. This is a sympathomimetic toxidrome. Central depression (C) is the opposite — that's depressant toxicity. This patient is "up," not "down." The next risk is seizures from CNS overstimulation, followed by cardiac dysrhythmias.

Step 3: Pattern Recognition

You don't need to identify the exact drug. You need to recognize the pattern.

A toxidrome is a recognizable cluster of signs and symptoms caused by a class of toxin. Each pattern tells you which body system is being targeted and what to expect next. Your BLS assessment findings — pupils, heart rate, respiratory rate, skin, LOC — are building a toxidrome profile every time you assess a patient.

Click each toxidrome to explore it:

💤 Opioid / Sedative ▼
SignsDecreased LOC, pinpoint pupils, slow/shallow respirations, hypotension, bradycardia
CausesFentanyl, heroin, morphine, oxycodone, benzodiazepines, alcohol
ExpectRespiratory arrest → airway obstruction → aspiration → cardiac arrest
KeyThe patient is "down." Everything is slowing. Protect the airway and ventilate.
⚡ Sympathomimetic ▼
SignsAgitation, dilated pupils, tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, seizures, diaphoresis
CausesCocaine, meth, amphetamines, MDMA/ecstasy
ExpectSeizures → cardiac dysrhythmias → stroke → hyperthermia → cardiac arrest
KeyThe patient is "up." Everything is accelerating. Prepare for seizures. Rapid transport.
💧 Cholinergic (SLUDGE) ▼
SignsSalivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis, bradycardia, miosis (constricted pupils)
CausesOrganophosphates, carbamates, nerve agents
ExpectBronchospasm → respiratory failure → seizures → bradycardia
KeyEverything is "wet." Secretions everywhere. Scene safety is critical — you can be secondarily exposed.
🔥 Anticholinergic ▼
SignsHot as a hare, blind as a bat (dilated pupils), dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter (delirium)

KeyEverything is "dry." The opposite of cholinergic. Hot, flushed, confused, dilated pupils — but no sweating.
⚠️ Challenge Your Assumptions

"I need to know the exact drug to manage a tox patient."

You don't. Is the patient "up" or "down"? "Wet" or "dry"? That pattern tells you which systems are under attack and what's coming next. That's what drives your management.

🧠 Check Yourself
You assess a patient with the following findings: hot, dry, flushed skin; dilated pupils; tachycardia; confused and picking at the air (visual hallucinations). An empty blister pack of diphenhydramine (Benadryl) is on the table. Which toxidrome does this match?
Hot, dry, dilated, confused — textbook anticholinergic. The key differentiator from sympathomimetic: the skin is dry. Sympathomimetic patients are diaphoretic (sweaty). Diphenhydramine in overdose is a potent anticholinergic. Anticipate seizures and dysrhythmias.
Look at the skin: dry. That's your differentiator. Sympathomimetic patients sweat (diaphoresis). This patient is hot but dry — "hot as a hare, dry as a bone, blind as a bat, red as a beet, mad as a hatter." Classic anticholinergic toxidrome. Diphenhydramine (an antihistamine) is a common cause.

Special Focus: Carbon Monoxide

The invisible killer that lies to your monitor.

BLS PCS p. 102, Guideline

📟 Scenario

You're called to a home in January for a "general illness." Three family members all have headaches and nausea. The furnace has been running all day. SpO₂ on all three patients reads 97–99%.

Would you trust those numbers?

Why CO is Dangerous

Carbon monoxide binds to haemoglobin with 200–250 times the affinity of oxygen. It forms carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb), which cannot carry oxygen. But it gets worse: CO also shifts the oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve to the left, meaning the remaining oxygenated haemoglobin holds onto its O₂ more tightly and won't release it to the tissues.

The result: the blood is full but the cells are starving.

⚠️ The Big Lie

"SpO₂ is 98%, so the patient isn't hypoxic."

Your pulse oximeter cannot tell the difference between oxyhaemoglobin and carboxyhaemoglobin. A patient can read 98% on the monitor and be profoundly hypoxic at the cellular level. Don't trust the number — trust the scene and the symptoms.

Scene Clues — Your Best Diagnostic Tool

The BLS emphasizes scene assessment for a reason. Look for:

ClueThink CO When…
Enclosed spaceGarage, basement, poorly ventilated room
Fuel-burning deviceFurnace, generator, car engine, space heater, fireplace
Multiple patientsSame symptoms in the same location = environmental cause
Seasonal patternHeating season (winter) — furnace malfunctions
Vague symptomsHeadache, nausea, weakness, dizziness — mimics flu, food poisoning, migraine
Clinical Pearl

CO poisoning mimics everything and presents as nothing specific. The BLS Guideline lists: altered mental status, cardiac dysrhythmias, emesis, headache, light-headedness, nausea, seizures, syncope, weakness, and VSA. If multiple patients in an enclosed space share vague symptoms — think CO until proven otherwise.

🧠 Check Yourself
Why does the BLS Tox Standard specifically mention "multiple patients" as a clue for carbon monoxide?
When several people in the same space develop similar non-specific symptoms (headache, nausea, weakness), it's a powerful clue that the environment is the cause, not an individual disease process. This is epidemiological thinking applied to the scene. Remove patients from the environment and treat with high-flow O₂.
CO isn't contagious — it's an environmental toxin. When multiple people in the same enclosed space develop similar vague symptoms, it points to a shared environmental exposure. That pattern recognition is your diagnostic tool when the monitor can't help you.

Your BLS Response Framework

The Tox Standard gives you a clear, systematic framework. Here's each action and the pathophysiology behind it.

ActionBLS ReferenceWhy
Identify agent, quantity, time, route Items 1–2, p. 101 Determines which systems are under attack, speed of onset, and what to anticipate
Refer to Altered LOC Standard if unconscious; protect airway Item 3, p. 101; ALOC p. 72 CNS depression = lost protective reflexes. OPA/NPA, suction, and positioning prevent aspiration
Prepare for cardiac arrest; CPR per Cardiac Arrest Standard Item 4(a), p. 101; Cardiac Arrest p. 74 Toxin-induced arrests are potentially reversible. Identify the cause early and communicate it
Manage airway obstruction; ventilate if respiratory failure Items 4(b–d), p. 101; Resp. Failure p. 95 Three mechanisms (central depression, obstruction, bronchospasm) — each needs different management
Prepare for seizures; position and protect Item 4(h), p. 101; Seizure p. 96 Many toxins lower seizure threshold. Seizure Standard includes "drug ingestion/withdrawal" as a cause
Consult poison control; refer to MSDS Guideline, p. 101 You're not expected to know every toxin. Use resources — but don't delay patient care or transport

Putting It All Together — Five Steps

Your Tox Call Framework

  1. Be the detective — identify agent, quantity, time, and route (BLS items 1–2)
  2. Recognize the pattern — is the patient UP (stimulant) or DOWN (depressant)? Build a toxidrome profile from your assessment findings
  3. Anticipate the cascade — CNS depression → lost reflexes → airway obstruction → respiratory arrest → cardiac arrest. Where is your patient on this timeline?
  4. Protect the airway above all else — whether the toxin is depressing breathing, causing emesis, or triggering seizures, the airway is always at risk
  5. Communicate what you know — agent, route, estimated dose, time of exposure are critical for ALS and the receiving facility to provide definitive care
📟 Return to Our Opening Call

0247h — 23-year-old male, GCS 6, snoring respirations, SpO₂ 91%, white powder at the scene.

Now apply the framework:

1. Detective: Unknown white powder (possible opioid/stimulant), route unknown (ingestion? insufflation? injection? — check for track marks, check nares), time ~30 minutes per bystander, quantity unknown.

2. Pattern: Patient is DOWN — decreased LOC, slow respirations. Pinpoint pupils? Check. This looks like opioid/sedative.

3. Cascade: Already at "lost protective reflexes" (GCS 6, snoring). Heading toward respiratory arrest if unchecked.

4. Airway: Open airway (head-tilt chin-lift or jaw thrust), suction, insert OPA/NPA, assist ventilations with BVM. Have suction ready for emesis.

5. Communicate: Suspected opioid overdose, GCS 6, assisted ventilations, administering naloxone PCP Scope. Advise receiving facility of estimated timeline and patient response to naloxone.

🧠 Check Yourself — Critical Thinking
A 19-year-old was found unresponsive at a party. Friends say she was drinking and "might have taken something." She's breathing at a rate of 6/min with pinpoint pupils. You begin BVM ventilations. Five minutes into transport, she suddenly seizes. What is the most important thing this tells you about the toxin?
Opioids alone rarely cause seizures (tramadol is an exception). Seizures in a patient presenting as "down" suggest a mixed ingestion — possibly opioids combined with something that lowers seizure threshold (e.g., tricyclic antidepressants, GHB, alcohol withdrawal). This changes your communication to ALS/hospital: "suspected polysubstance ingestion with mixed presentation." Never assume it's one drug.
Pure opioid overdose rarely causes seizures (with the exception of tramadol). When a patient who looks "opioid" suddenly seizes, it should make you reconsider: could this be a mixed ingestion? Something that causes both CNS depression and seizure activity (e.g., TCAs, GHB, alcohol withdrawal, tramadol)? This changes your communication to ALS and the receiving facility.

5 Must-Know Takeaways

Tox Summary Card — Keep This

  1. Toxins don't invent new problems — they attack the same 4 systems (CNS, respiratory, cardiovascular, GI/thermoregulation). Your BLS assessment already covers all of them.
  2. Route determines speed — injection/inhalation = seconds. Ingestion = minutes to hours. Extended-release = a delayed trap.
  3. "Up or Down?" — is the patient stimulated or depressed? This single question identifies the toxidrome pattern and predicts the cascade.
  4. Airway is always the priority — whether the patient is seizing, vomiting, or going unconscious, the airway is the common threat. Suction ready, positioned, protected.
  5. Communicate what you know — agent, route, dose, time. You don't need a diagnosis. You need to relay intelligence that enables definitive care downstream.

🧠 Scenario-Based Integration
You respond to a farm call. A 52-year-old agricultural worker was spraying crops and is now found with excessive salivation, tearing, has urinated and defecated on himself, and is vomiting. He's bradycardic at 48 bpm with laboured, wheezy breathing. His pupils are constricted. What is the most critical action beyond standard BLS airway management?
SLUDGE — classic cholinergic toxidrome from organophosphate exposure. This is the one toxidrome where scene safety is paramount: the same agent that's killing the patient can contaminate you through skin contact. Decontamination matters. Request ALS early — the definitive treatment is atropine (ACP-scope ACP), which blocks the acetylcholine flood. Your BLS priorities: scene safety, decontamination, airway management (these patients drown in their own secretions), and rapid ALS intercept/transport.
