Start Here — A Call That Matters

Before we talk anatomy, walk through this call. It'll change how you read the rest of this module.

Dispatch

You're dispatched to a long-term care home for an 81-year-old female, "confusion — staff reports patient not acting like herself." Low priority. Staff says she's been "off" for about a week.

On Scene

You find Margaret sitting in a wheelchair. GCS 14 (E4 V4 M6). She's oriented to person only. Staff says she's usually sharp — plays cards, remembers everyone's name. Over the past week: increasingly confused, unsteady, stopped going to the dining hall. Her doctor visited 3 days ago and ordered bloodwork for ?UTI, ?dementia.

Key Finding

You check her medication list: apixaban 5mg BID. You ask staff about falls — "Oh, she had a little tumble about two weeks ago getting out of bed. Didn't even hit her head that hard. She seemed fine after."

The Question

What are you thinking? This isn't a UTI call anymore. Elderly + anticoagulated + fall (even minor, even 2 weeks ago) + progressive confusion = chronic subdural hematoma until proven otherwise. The doctor's dementia workup may have missed it. Your history-taking just changed this patient's trajectory.

This is why you're here. Margaret's story is the chronic SDH that gets missed every day. Let's learn why it happens and how to catch it.

The Why — What's Happening Inside the Skull

You don't need to be a neurosurgeon. You need to understand enough to recognize the threat and explain your clinical reasoning.

The Meninges — Three Layers, One Matters Most

The brain is wrapped in three membrane layers: dura mater (tough outer layer), arachnoid (middle), and pia mater (inner, sits on the brain). Between the dura and arachnoid is the subdural space — and that's where the problem is.

Bridging veins cross this space, connecting the brain surface to dural drainage channels. They're small, fragile, and the source of subdural bleeding.

Venous vs. Arterial — Why SDH Is Sneaky

Epidural hematomas are arterial — fast, high-pressure, dramatic. Subdurals are venous — slower, lower pressure. But the blood spreads like a thin sheet across the entire brain surface instead of forming one focused clot. The danger isn't speed — it's coverage and the false sense of security a "stable" patient gives you.

The Monro-Kellie Doctrine — The Rule That Explains Everything

The skull is a fixed box containing three things: brain, blood, and CSF. Total volume cannot change. Add something (hematoma) → something else gets squeezed.

1 CSF pushed out first →
2 Venous blood compressed →
3 Brain compressed → rising ICP →
4 Herniation → death

This is why your patient looks "fine" in Stage 1 — the brain is compensating by pushing CSF out. Once that buffer is gone, pressure rises exponentially. Every assessment concept in this module ties back to this doctrine.

Acute vs. Chronic — Two Different Animals

ACUTE SDH

Minutes to hours. Significant trauma. Rapid decline. Classic herniation progression. Mortality 50-90% without surgery.

CHRONIC SDH

Days to weeks. Minor or forgotten trauma. Subtle — confusion, personality changes, unsteadiness. The one you'll miss.

High Risk — The Patients Who Need Your Suspicion

Knowing who's at risk changes how you approach the call before you even touch the patient.

👴
Elderly

Brain shrinks with age → bridging veins stretch across a wider gap → more tension → tear more easily. A minor bump can bleed.

💊
Anticoagulated

Warfarin, DOACs (rivaroxiban, apixaban, dabigatran), ASA, clopidogrel — impaired clotting means even small tears bleed more and longer.

🧠
Alcoholic

Alcohol causes brain atrophy (same stretching problem as aging) PLUS liver damage impairs clotting factor production. Double risk.

👶
Infants

Bridging veins proportionally larger and more fragile. Always consider non-accidental trauma — violent shaking tears bridging veins.

BLS Anchor (p116): The Head Injury standard tells you to consider intracranial hemorrhage as a threat. Now you know WHY a "minor fall" in a 78-year-old on warfarin is NOT minor. Ask about medications. Ask about falls. Ask about timeline.

Assessment — What to Look For and Why It Matters

Every observation in the BLS Head Injury Standard (p116-117) is a checkpoint in a progression. Here's what each finding tells you.

History — Ask These, Every Time

Mechanism & timing
Even minor? How long ago? Separates acute from chronic.
Medications
Anticoagulants change everything. Always ask.
Time course
Sudden = acute. Gradual over days/weeks = chronic.
Recent falls or bumps
Elderly may minimize or forget. Ask family/staff.
Baseline mental status
Is this confusion new? Compare to their normal.

Physical Findings — What They Mean

CSF from ears/nose
Skull fracture created a path outward. Significant trauma. Don't plug it — it's reducing ICP.
Battle's sign
Blood tracking from basilar skull fracture. Takes hours — injury is older than reported.
Raccoon eyes
Blood tracking forward from basilar fracture. Late sign = significant trauma.
Abnormal posturing
Brain compression. Decorticate (arms flexed) = above brainstem. Decerebrate (arms extended) = brainstem. Decerebrate is WORSE — herniation.
Agitation / fluctuating
Hematoma pressing on brain. Fluctuation = bleeding active or compensation failing.
Incontinence
Brain's inhibitory centres compressed. Significant dysfunction.
Vomiting
Rising ICP stimulates vomiting centre. BLS Nausea/Vomiting Standard (p94) lists 'increased ICP' as a threat.

Vitals & GCS — The Numbers That Talk

GCS trend: A DROPPING GCS is your most important finding. Single numbers mean nothing — track the trend. GCS <9 + herniation signs = hyperventilate.
Pupils: Size, equality, reactivity. Unilateral blown pupil = brain herniating on that side (CN III compression).
BP + HR: Rising BP + falling HR = Cushing's response. The brain is forcing blood past rising pressure. LATE sign.
Respiratory pattern: Irregular breathing = brainstem compression. Prepare for respiratory arrest.

The Clock — How SDH Progresses

Your job is to recognize WHERE on this timeline the patient is and act before they move to the next stage. Serial assessment every 5 minutes is your early warning system.

Stage 1Compensation
Patient Signs

Headache, mild confusion, nausea. Vitals may be normal. GCS 14-15.

What You See on the Truck

The patient looks okay. They're talking to you. It would be easy to dismiss this.

⚠ THE DANGER ZONE: The brain is compensating by pushing CSF out. The 'lucid interval' lives here — the patient seems fine right up until they're not. Once compensation is exhausted, pressure rises exponentially.
Stage 2Decompensation
Patient Signs

Increasing confusion, agitation, fluctuating LOC, vomiting, worsening headache. GCS dropping. Pupils sluggish or unequal.

What You See on the Truck

Your serial GCS catches this. The patient who was GCS 14 is now GCS 11. Cushing's response may appear: hypertension + bradycardia + irregular respirations.

Your assessments are catching the decline. This patient is on the edge. Be ready for rapid progression to Stage 3.
Stage 3Herniation
Patient Signs

GCS <9 + dilated/unreactive pupils OR asymmetric pupils OR decorticate/decerebrate posturing. Breathing irregular or stops.

What You See on the Truck

BLS p117 herniation criteria met. Hyperventilate to ETCO2 30-35 mmHg. This patient needs a surgeon. Everything you do now is buying time.

WITHOUT SURGICAL INTERVENTION, THE PATIENT WILL DIE. Hyperventilation is a bridge, not a treatment.

Management — What to Do and Why

Every intervention has a physiological rationale. Knowing the "why" means you'll adapt when the textbook scenario doesn't match reality.

Airway & Ventilation
Ventilate if apneic or inadequate. ETCO2 35-45 mmHg normally.
Why: The brain needs oxygen. Hypoxia accelerates brain swelling and herniation. Normal CO2 keeps cerebral blood vessels at normal size = normal ICP.
If Herniation Signs Present
Hyperventilate to ETCO2 30-35 mmHg. Adult ~20/min, Child ~25/min, Infant ~30/min.
Why: Lowering CO2 → cerebral vasoconstriction → blood vessels shrink → reduces blood volume in skull → temporarily drops ICP. This is a BRIDGE to surgery, not a cure.
DO NOT Go Below ETCO2 30
Watch your capnography closely.
Why: Excessive vasoconstriction = brain ischemia. You're trying to lower pressure, not starve the brain of blood.
Position: Head Up 30°
Elevate head 30 degrees. If on spinal board, tilt the whole board.
Why: Gravity helps venous blood drain out of the skull, reducing ICP. Simple but effective.
CSF Leak
Loose sterile dressing. Do NOT plug it.
Why: CSF leaking out is actually reducing ICP. Prevent infection but don't block drainage.
Seizure Preparedness
Anticipate seizures. Have suction ready.
Why: Blood irritates brain tissue. The hematoma on the cortex can trigger seizures, which increase metabolic demand and worsen ICP.
Transport
Load and Go. This patient needs a surgeon, not your ambulance.
Why: No field intervention fixes this. Everything you do is buying time for definitive surgical intervention. Don't delay transport for procedures you can do en route.
What NOT To Do
  • Don't drop BP aggressively — the body is raising BP to perfuse the brain. Drop BP = brain loses blood flow.
  • Don't delay transport for interventions that can be done en route.
  • Don't assume "minor fall" is minor in elderly or anticoagulated patients.

The Traps — Myths, Mimics & Missed Calls

Challenge what you think you know. These are the assumptions that lead to missed SDHs.

Myth Busters — Tap to Reveal

🚫 Myth: "Minor falls don't cause brain bleeds."
▼ Tap
REALITY

In elderly or anticoagulated patients, even a bump against a cupboard door can tear stretched bridging veins. The #1 cause of SDH is falls — including ones the patient calls "nothing."

🚫 Myth: "If the patient is talking and alert, they're fine."
▼ Tap
REALITY

The lucid interval exists because the brain compensates — CSF gets pushed out, venous blood compresses. The patient looks fine right up until compensation fails and they crash. A single GCS of 15 means nothing without serial reassessment.

🚫 Myth: "Hyperventilating a head injury is always right."
▼ Tap
REALITY

Only hyperventilate with confirmed herniation signs (GCS <9 + pupil/posturing changes). Routine hyperventilation in head injuries causes cerebral vasoconstriction and can worsen brain ischemia. Normal target: ETCO2 35-45 mmHg.
